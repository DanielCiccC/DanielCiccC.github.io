Arbitrage: transactions

To show why K = S + I + J − D must hold, consider the following arbitrage arguments:

Suppose K>S+I+J−DK > S + I + J − DK>S+I+J−D and consider the following short trade: Transactions at time t = 0:

  • Borrow SSS to buy 1 unit of the underlying asset spot.
  • Short 1 contract to sell the asset for KKK at maturity TTT.

Note that your net cashflow at time t=0t = 0t=0 is 0, since the money you received from borrowing was used to buy the asset.

Transactions at maturity, time T:

  • Receive K for selling the asset in the contract.
  • Pay off the loan S with interest I.
  • Pay J for owning, holding and storing the asset.
  • Receive any other income D from owning the asset.

Then your net cashflow at maturity is positive:

K−S−I−J+D>0K − S − I − J + D > 0K−S−I−J+D>0

Keep borrowing to buy the asset and short the contract for no initial net cashflow, but receive the positive net cashflow at maturity: An arbitrage.

Remark Another way to look at it is in terms of contract value. If K>S+I+J−DK > S + I + J − DK>S+I+J−D then your short trade locks in the positive cashflow of K−S−I−J+DK − S − I − J + DK−S−I−J+D at maturity.

  • This cashflow is thus risk free and its value

is positive to you and negative to the long position.

  • You’d need to compensate the long position by this amount. But K is set so the contract has 0 initial value to both parties.

Transactions at time t=0t = 0t=0:

Cost of Carry

We thus get the cost of carry model for pricing forwards and futures:

K=S+I+J−DK = S + I + J − DK=S+I+J−D

Here I+J−DI + J − DI+J−D is the net cost of carrying (holding, storing) the asset.

Remark

The cost of carry model is usually written with annual borrowing rates rrr, storage rates sss and dividend or convenience yields qqq, and is called spot-forward parity since it defines the relation between spot and forward prices

  • normally written in terms of borrowing rates
  • normally called spot forward parody

Let rrr be a simple annual interest rate, sss be a simple annual storage rate, and qqq be a simple annual dividend (or later convenience) yield.

  • Then the cost of carrying the asset is I+J−D=SrT+SsT−SqT.I + J − D = SrT + SsT − SqT.I+J−D=SrT+SsT−SqT.
  • The cost of carry relation K=S+I+J−DK = S + I + J − DK=S+I+J−D becomes

K=S[1+(r+s−q)T]K = S[1 + (r + s − q)T]K=S[1+(r+s−q)T]

and is called spot-forward parity. Under compound interest it becomes

Simple interest

K=S(1+r+s−q)TK = S(1 + r + s − q)^{T}K=S(1+r+s−q)T

or (Compound Interest)

K=Se(r+s−q)TK = Se^{(r+s−q)T}K=Se(r+s−q)T

What is the relation between spot S and forward K prices?

From:

K=Se(r+s−q)TK = Se^{(r+s−q)T}K=Se(r+s−q)T

if the cost of carry r+s−qr + s − qr+s−q (in rates) is:

  • Positive, so r+s−q>0r + s − q > 0r+s−q>0 then K>SK > SK>S (forward prices are greater than spot prices) and we say the market is normal.
  • Negative, so r+s−q<0r + s − q < 0r+s−q<0 then K<SK < SK<S (forward prices are less than spot prices) and we say the market is inverted.

Commodity contract pricing

In the case of commodity futures and forwards, we have the:

  • Interest rate rrr on borrowing and lending.
  • Carrying cost sss representing storage and insurance, etc, costs.
  • Convenience yield qqq representing benefits of owning the asset:
    • Wholesalers maintain adequate stock levels to supply their customers in a timely and reliable manner.
    • Manufacturers maintain adequate stock in order to use/consume and keep their production processes running and not get held up.

So the cost-of-carry model and spot-forward parity relation is as above.

  • People are willing to pay a premium to have stock in inventory
  • Receive an abstract benefit for keeping their supplies in stock
    • Quantified in commodity futures
  • Cost of carry model

FX contract pricing

Spot-forward parity for FX contracts is best derived separately. The main complications are that we have to consider the interest rates in each country and we have to be careful about exchange rate quoting.

  • Let rdr_{d}rd​ be domestic interest rate and
  • rfr_{f}rf​ be the foreign rate.
  • Let Sd:fS_{d:f}Sd:f​ be the domestic:foreign spot exchange rate.
    • 1 unit of the domestic currency exchanges spot for Sd:fS_{d:f}Sd:f​ units of the foreign currency.
  • Let Kd:fK_{d:f}Kd:f​ be the domestic:foreign forward exchange rate.
  • currency contracts - instead of using cost of carry, best to derive the currency forward price/forward exchange rate separately

Two ways of earning interest over a time period T, namely:

  1. Invest domestically at rdr_{d}rd​ to get 1+rdT1 + r_{d}T1+rd​T per unit invested.
  2. Invest internationally:
  • Buy Sd:fS_{d:f}Sd:f​ units of foreign currency spot and simultaneously lock in the futures exchange rate of Kf:dK_{f:d}Kf:d​ at time TTT.
  • Invest Sd:fS_{d:f}Sd:f​ at rfr_{f}rf​ to get Sd:f(1+rfT)S_{d:f} (1 + r_{f}T)Sd:f​(1+rf​T).
  • Exchange this amount back into the domestic currency via the futures contract to end up with Sd:f(1+rfT)Kf:dS_{d:f} (1 + r_{f}T) K_{f:d}Sd:f​(1+rf​T)Kf:d​

Both avenues must have the same final value or else there’s an arbitrage opportunity: Invest in the best avenue fund it with a reverse transaction (borrowing) in the other avenue.

  • carry trade, exchange domestic currency
  • investing domestically has to equal investing internationally through the carry trade

Hence, the no arbitrage relation is 1+rdT=Sd:f(1+rfT)Kf:d1 + r_{d}T = S_{d:f} (1 + r_{f}T) K_{f:d}1+rd​T=Sd:f​(1+rf​T)Kf:d​ which we rearrange to get the covered interest rate parity relation

Recall that a T1×T2T_{1} \times T_{2}T1​×T2​ FRA is an OTC agreement to lock in a future borrowing or lending fixed rate kkk starting at time T1T_{1}T1​ , and finishing at time T2T_{2}T2​ over a notional principal or face value F.

  • Time T1T_{1}T1​ is the FRA’s maturity date.
  • The length of the time period is given by T=T2−T1T = T_2 − T_1T=T2​−T1​ .
  • The fixed rate receiver hypothetically agrees to invest FFF at time T1T_{1}T1​ and receive the cashflow F(1+kT)F(1 + kT)F(1+kT) at time T2T_{2}T2​ .
  • The fixed rate payer hypothetically agrees to borrow F at time T1T_{1}T1​ and pay off the loan amount of F(1+kT)F(1 + kT)F(1+kT) at time T2T_{2}T2​.
  • FRA are cash settled at maturity, time T1T_{1}T1​.
  • FRA are written over reference rates such as SOFR or EURIBOR.

Fixed rate receiver payoff

where rrr is the spot rate at maturity over the period [T1,T2][T_{1}, T_{2}][T1​,T2​] of length T.

Optimal Hedging

Last week we presented “perfect” hedging scenarios in which the:

  • Underlying asset of a futures/forward contract is precisely the asset we want to hedge an exposure to,
  • Number of assets we held was divisible by the contract multiplier m, enabling us to use an integer number of contracts.
  • Contract maturity date is precisely our desired hedging date.

Perfect hedging scenarios are rare in practice:

So far presented unrealistic hedging

  • Large diversified share portfolio, there is no futures contract that can perfectly hedge
  • not exactly over the asset that we hold
  • contract multiplier might not be divisible
  • date might not be the exact maturity date
  • Use some sort of futures contract

Basis Risk

We saw above that the contract price K is usually different to the spot price S of the underlying asset. Our pricing equations, such as

K=Se(r+s−q)TK=Se^{(r+s-q)T}K=Se(r+s−q)T

for commodity futures, tell us that the diff erence between KKK and SSS, which we call the basis, is due to the cost of carry r+s−qr + s − qr+s−q.

  • As the cost of carry changes over time, the basis changes, introducing basis risk: KKK may not be perfectly correlated with SSS.

Remark The situation is even more complicated if the asset we hold and want to hedge is not the same as the contract’s underlying asset.

  • interest rates, storage costs change every day
  • difference between contract price and spot price
    • when we do perfect hedging

The time ttt basis BtB_tBt​ is the difference between KtK_tKt​ and StS_tSt​, namely:

Bt=Kt−StB_{t} = K_{t}-S_{t}Bt​=Kt​−St​

Note that the basis approaches 0 over time, and KT=STK_{T}=S_{T}KT​=ST​ at maturity.

Optimal hedging

Optimal hedging basically means minimising basis risk. Suppose:

  • We hold QQQ units in an asset at time t=0t = 0t=0.
    • Its price at time ttt is denoted by ATA_{T}AT​.
  • We want to sell our holding at time ttt.
    • We want to hedge our exposure to a fall in the asset price.
  • There is a futures contract maturing at time TTT, with t<Tt < Tt<T.
    • The underlying asset of the futures contract SSS may not necessarily be the same as the asset A we hold.
  • We short hhh units of the futures contract at time t=0t = 0t=0 for KKK.
  • We sell our asset holding and close out the futures position at time ttt.

How many contracts h should we short?

  • minimise basis risk
  • Hypothetical time t=0
  • exposed to the price of the asset falling
    • asset to short to hedge this risk
    • may not necessarily be on the same date
  • We still sell the contract, even if they might not be exactly the same (but are correlated)
    • probably use the contract to hedge exposure
    • given all uncertainties (basis risk)
